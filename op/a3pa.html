A3Pa: Artaxerxes III, Persepolis inscription A
A smoothed stone wall, next to a stone staircase, decorated with 35 lines of cuneiform. This particular text was transcribed and translated directly by me from a high-resolution color photo, which was kindly provided to me directly by Jona Lendering. Lower-quality copies of these photos can also be found on Jona's own website, Livius. I thank Jona for kindly providing me with these photos.
As with all Old Persian translations, Roland G. Kent's "Old Persian, Grammar, Texts and Lexicon" (1950), was used as a source for translating words. I am not a linguist, and this translation was only done out of personal interest and passion for ancient Middle-Eastern languages.
ba[ga vaz]raka auramazdā ha[ya] imām bumām a[d]ā [haya] avam asmānām [ad]ā haya šāyatām adā martihaya haya mām [a]rtaxšaçā xšāyathiya [a]kunauš aivam paruvanā[m] xšāyathiyam aivam paruvanām framatāram
A great god is Auramazda, he made this earth, he made that sky, he made man, he made happiness for man, he made me, Artaxerxes, king, one king of many, one lord of many.
[th]atiy artaxšaçǎ xš[āya]thiya vazraka xšāya[th]iya xšāyathiyanām xšāyathiya dahayāyunām xšāyathiya ahayāyā bumiya adam artaxšaçā xšāyathiya puça artaxšaçā dārayavauš xšāyathiya puça dārayavauš artaxšaçā [x]šāyathiya puça artaxšaçā xšayāršā xšāyathiya puça xšayāršā dārayavauš xšāyathiya puça dārayavauš vištāspahayā nāma puça vištāspahayā aršām nāma puça haxǎmanišiya
Artaxerxes, the great king, king of kings, king of the lands, king on this earth, declares: I am the son of king Artaxerxes, Artaxerxes the son of king Darius, Darius the son of king Artaxerxes, Artaxerxes the son of king Xerxes, Xerxes the son of king Darius, Darius the son of one named Vishtaspa, Vishtaspa the son of one named Arsames, the Achaemenid.
thātiy artaxšaçā xšāyathiya imam ustašanām athaganām mām upā mām kartā
Artaxerxes the king declares: this staircase of stone, I have had made under me.
thātiy artaxšaçā xšāyathiya mām auramazdā utā mithra baga pāntuv utā imām dahayaum utā taya mām kartā
Artaxerxes the king declares: may Auramazda and the god Mithra protect me and these lands and that which I have made.
Comments
A large crach on the surface of the inscription makes some characters at the beginning on lines 1-16 partially to fully illegible. As indicated with square brackets, these characters were reconstructed using educated guesswork.
Interestingly, this inscription suffers from a number of spelling changes or mistakes when comparing this to older texts, such as DB. I have left these changes in the transcriptions as they are written, but will list some obvious ones here. [3] bumām is assumed to be a misspelling of bumim (= bumiš [asm]). This may be a simple errornous substitution of -a- for -i-, or a later declension for the i-stems to be more in line with the a-stems. As the form is only attested here, this hypothesis cannot be definitively concluded from this text alone. [5] šāyatām is assumed to be a misspelling of šiyatām. [13] xšāyathiyanām is assumed to be a misspelling of xšāyathiyānām (= xšāyathiya [gpm]). [24] The name vištāspahayā appears to be used as a nominative, while in DB I.4, this same form was used as genitive. [34] the word pāntuv is spelled as pāntūv, with an extra -u- symbol.
When comparing this text to AsH, we note that Artaxerxes does not refer to his predecessor Arsames as being a king, whereas the tablet itself claims to be written by "king Arsames" himself. This would support the theory that this tablet would be a fake.
