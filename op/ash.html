AsH: Arsames, Hamadan
A golden tablet, excavated in Hamadan, Iran, in 1920. The tablet contains 14 lines of Old Persian cuneiform inscription, and is attributed to Arsames, son of Ariaramnes, and grandfather to the later king Darius I of the Achaemenid empire.
As with all Old Persian translations, the text is sourced from Roland G. Kent's "Old Persian, Grammar, Texts and Lexicon" (1950), which was also used as a source for translating words. I am not a linguist, and this translation was only done out of personal interest and passion for ancient Middle-Eastern languages.
arshāma xshāyathiya vazrka xshāyathiya xshāyathiyānām pārsa ariyāramna xshāyathiyahya puça haxāmanishiya
I am Arsames, the great king, king of kings, king in Persia, son of king Ariaramnes, the Achaemenid.
thātiy arshām xshāyathiya auramazdā baga vazrka mathishta bagānām mām xshāyathiyam akunaush hauv dahyāum pārsam manā frābara taya ukāram uvaspam vashnā auramazdāha imām dahyāum dārayāmiy mām auramazdā pātuv utāmaiy vitham utā imām dahyāum taya adam dārayāmiy hauv pātuv
King Arsames says: A great god is Auramazda, the greatest of the gods, he made me king, he gave me the land Persia, that with good men and with good horses, by the will of Auramazda, I hold this land, may Auramazda protect me and my house and may he protect this land that I hold.
Comments
This very brief text is claimed to be written by Arsames, son of Ariaramnes, and grandfather of the later Darius I. Interestingly, Arsames claims to be
king of kingsxshāyathiya xshāyathiyānām
, and that his father was
kingxshāyathiya
before him, yet Darius later would (in the Behistun inscription) identify himself as the only ruler from the Ariaramnes branch of the Achaemenid family (identifying the branch of Cyrus I, brother of Ariaramnes as the line of succession to Achaemenes). Darius would also not use the epithet
kingxshāyathiya
for either his father or grandfather.
This text is very straightforward and contains very few features which I consider interesting. The only brief comment I would like to make is the disctinction between the common use of the genitive in for example
xshāyathiya dahyunāmking of the lands
in XV.11-12 or DB.I.2, as opposed to the locative which Arsames uses in
xshāyathiya pārsāking in Persia
. I have reflected this distinction by translating it as king in Persia rather than king of Persia. I would have expected a genitive here, as I feel was more the norm. The meaning however, is quite the same, and its use is not completely nonsensical.
