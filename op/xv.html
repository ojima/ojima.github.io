XV, Xerxes I at Van
A wall inscription attributed to Xerxes I (c. 518 - 465 BC), king of kings of the Achaemenid empire. The inscription is found at the fortress near Van, modern-day Turkey.
As with all Old Persian translations, the text is sourced from Roland G. Kent's "Old Persian, Grammar, Texts and Lexicon" (1950), which was also used as a source for translating words. I am not a linguist, and this translation was only done out of personal interest and passion for ancient Middle-Eastern languages.
baga vazrka auramazdā hya mathišta bagānām hya imām bumim adā hya avam asmānam adā hya mrtiyam adā hya šiyātim adā mrtiyam adā hya šiyātim adā mrtiyahya hya xšayāršām xšāyathiyam akunauš aivam parunam xšayathiyam aivam parunam frmātāram
A great god is Auramazda, the greatest of the gods, he created this earth, he created that sky, he created man, he created happiness for man, he made Xerxes king, one king of many, one lord of many.
adam xšayāršā xšāyathiya vazrka xšāyathiya xšāyathiyānām xšāyathiya dahyunām paruv zanānām xšāyathiya ahyāyā bumiyā vazrkāyā duraiy apiy dārayavahauš xšāyathiyahya puça haxāmanišiya
I am Xerxes, the great king, king of kings, king of the lands with many peoples, king on this great earth far and wide, son of king Darius the Achaemenid.
thātiy xšayārša xšāyathiya dārayavauš xšāyathiya hya manā pitā hauv vašnā auramazdāha vasiy tya naibam akunauš utā ima stānam hauv niyaštāya katanaiy yaniy dipim naiy nipištām akunauš pasāva adam niyaštāyam imām dipim nipaištanaiy mām auramazdā pātuv hadā bagaibiš utāmaiy xšaçam utā tyamaiy krtam
King Xerxes says: king Darius, my father, he, by the will of Auramazda, did much good, and this place he commanded to be dug out, at this inscription, he did not engrave, afterwards I commanded this inscription to be engraved, may Auramazda, with the gods, protect me and my kingdom and my work.
Comments
The text itself is quite interesting (I actually find it funny) since it can be summarized as Xerxes telling us: "my father Darius made this wall but he wrote nothing here, so I wrote this here". It is the only Achaemenid royal inscription outside modern-day Iran. The original text exists (similar to DB) in three variants: one in Old Persian, one in Babylonian and one in Elamite, with the Old Persian text taking up the most space.
Between 1938 and 1940, an American scholar, Silva Tipple New Lake, led an expedition to the Urartu region in eastern Turkey. The expedition also explored the citadel at Van, but some the finds and field records from this expedition were all lost when they were transported in 1939 aboard the S.S. Athenia, which was sunk by a German U-boat in the Atlantic, two days after the start of World War II.
